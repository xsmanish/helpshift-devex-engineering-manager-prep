# Performance Management

## Helpshift Engineering Manager L3 Interview Preparation

---

# Why This Topic Matters

One of the biggest transitions from Senior Engineer to Engineering Manager is realizing that success is no longer measured by your individual output.

Your success is measured by:

* Team performance
* Team growth
* Delivery outcomes
* Employee engagement
* Talent retention
* Leadership pipeline

Interviewers at Helpshift will want to understand:

* How you manage underperformance
* How you handle difficult conversations
* How you coach engineers
* How you evaluate promotions
* How you manage high performers
* How you handle toxic behavior
* How you build accountability

The biggest mistake candidates make is treating performance management as a yearly HR exercise.

Great managers treat performance management as a continuous coaching process.

---

# My Performance Management Philosophy

## Core Belief

Performance management should never be a surprise.

Engineers should always know:

* What is expected
* How they are performing
* What they are doing well
* What needs improvement
* What is required for growth

---

---

### Potential

---

### Behavior

---

All three matter.

A person delivering strong results while damaging team culture is not a successful employee.

# Scenario Question

## How Do You Manage Underperformance?

This is one of the most common L3 questions.

---

# Strong Answer Structure

### Step 1

Understand the problem.

### Step 2

Identify root cause.

### Step 3

Create improvement plan.

### Step 4

Provide support.

### Step 5

Measure progress.

### Step 6

Escalate if necessary.

---

# Sample Answer

When I observe underperformance, I first focus on understanding the root cause rather than jumping to conclusions.

I schedule a private discussion and seek to understand what has changed.

The issue may be related to:

* Skills
* Motivation
* Burnout
* Personal challenges
* Role mismatch
* Organizational blockers

Once the root cause is understood, I create a clear improvement plan with measurable goals, provide support, and establish regular checkpoints.

My goal is always to help the employee succeed before considering formal performance processes.

---

# Real Interview Story

## Situation

One engineer who had consistently performed well began missing commitments across multiple projects.

---

## Task

Identify the cause and improve performance.

---

## Action

I conducted one-on-one discussions and reviewed workload distribution.

We discovered the engineer was supporting multiple teams simultaneously and was constantly context switching.

I worked with stakeholders to reduce competing priorities and created a focused execution plan.

---

## Result

Within two sprints:

* Delivery improved
* Stress reduced
* Stakeholder confidence returned

The problem was not capability.

The problem was organizational overload.

# Root Cause Framework

Whenever performance issues arise, I classify them into one of five categories.

---

## Category 1: Skill Gap

Employee lacks required capability.

Examples:

* New technology
* System design
* Communication

Solution:

* Training
* Mentoring
* Pairing

---

## Category 2: Will Gap

Employee has capability but lacks motivation.

Examples:

* Disengagement
* Lack of ownership

Solution:

* Career discussions
* Better alignment
* Meaningful work

---

## Category 3: Role Mismatch

Wrong person in wrong role.

Example:

Strong IC promoted into leadership without interest in management.

Solution:

Role realignment.

---

## Category 4: Burnout

Common among high performers.

Signs:

* Reduced engagement
* Missed deadlines
* Emotional fatigue

Solution:

Reduce workload and improve balance.

---

## Category 5: Organizational Issues

Performance blocked by external factors.

Examples:

* Too many priorities
* Unclear goals
* Poor processes

Solution:

Manager removes obstacles.

# Scenario Question

## How Do You Give Difficult Feedback?

---

# Sample Answer

I provide feedback early, privately, and with specific examples.

I focus on behavior and impact rather than personality.

Instead of saying:

"You are not collaborative."

I say:

"In the last architecture review, several team members felt their input was dismissed. This reduced participation and slowed alignment."

Specific feedback is actionable.

General criticism is not.

# Scenario Question

## What If a High Performer Wants a Promotion?

---

# Strong Answer

I separate:

### Strong Performance

From

### Promotion Readiness

Promotion requires evidence of operating consistently at the next level.

I explain:

* Current strengths
* Gaps
* Development plan
* Timeline

Promotion decisions should never surprise employees.

---

# Real Example

## Situation

A strong engineer requested promotion to Staff Engineer.

---

## Action

We reviewed expectations together.

The engineer demonstrated technical excellence but lacked organizational influence and mentoring experience.

I created opportunities for:

* Cross-team architecture reviews
* Technical leadership
* Mentoring

---

## Result

Six months later the engineer successfully demonstrated Staff-level behaviors and earned promotion.

# Scenario Question

## What If Someone Disagrees With Their Performance Review?

---

# Sample Answer

I focus on evidence.

I review:

* Goals
* Deliverables
* Feedback
* Impact

I encourage discussion and listen carefully.

Performance reviews should be based on observable behaviors and outcomes, not opinions.

Even when disagreements remain, transparency builds trust.

---

# Handling Toxic High Performers

This is an extremely common leadership interview question.

---

# Scenario

A brilliant engineer delivers exceptional results but damages team morale.

What do you do?

---

# Strong Answer

I would address the behavior directly.

Performance is not only about results.

It is also about how results are achieved.

If someone consistently damages collaboration, trust, or team culture, intervention is required regardless of technical contribution.

I would:

* Provide feedback
* Set expectations
* Create improvement plan

If behavior does not improve, I would escalate through formal performance management.

No individual is more important than team health.

---

# STAR Story

## Situation

A technically strong contributor frequently dismissed ideas from other engineers.

---

## Task

Improve team collaboration.

---

## Action

I conducted multiple coaching sessions focused on communication, influence, and leadership expectations.

I shared examples and explained impact.

---

## Result

The engineer became more collaborative and team participation improved significantly.

# Scenario Question

## How Do You Build Accountability?

---

# Sample Answer

Accountability starts with clarity.

People cannot be accountable for unclear expectations.

I focus on:

* Clear ownership
* Clear goals
* Measurable outcomes
* Regular follow-ups

Accountability should be part of team culture rather than something enforced through fear.

---

# Performance Improvement Plans (PIP)

Interviewers may ask about this.

---

# My View

A PIP should not be a surprise.

By the time formal performance management begins:

* Expectations have been discussed
* Feedback has been provided
* Coaching has occurred
* Improvement opportunities have been offered

The goal should always be success rather than documentation.

---

# Scenario Question

## Have You Ever Had to Exit Someone?

### Sample Answer

I have been involved in performance management situations where employees were unable to meet expectations despite coaching and support.

My approach is always:

* Fairness
* Transparency
* Documentation
* Respect

If separation becomes necessary, it should be handled professionally and with dignity.

The goal is not punishment.

The goal is maintaining standards while treating people respectfully.
