# Hiring and Talent Management

## Helpshift Engineering Manager L3 Interview Preparation

---

# Why This Topic Matters

As an Engineering Manager, your long-term impact is determined less by the code you write and more by the people you hire, develop, and retain.

A mediocre hiring decision can affect delivery, team morale, culture, and customer outcomes for years.

A strong hiring decision can elevate an entire organization.

Interviewers at Helpshift will evaluate:

* How you hire
* How you evaluate talent
* How you build teams
* How you retain top performers
* How you manage low performers
* How you grow future leaders
* How you make difficult people decisions

---

# My Hiring Philosophy

## Core Belief

Great teams are built intentionally.

I hire for:

1. Capability
2. Ownership
3. Learning Agility
4. Collaboration
5. Cultural Contribution

I do not hire solely based on technical depth.

Many technical gaps can be coached.

Poor attitude, lack of ownership, and inability to collaborate are much harder to fix.

---

# The Cost of a Bad Hire

Bad hires impact:

### Delivery

* Missed commitments
* Reduced productivity

### Team Morale

* Increased frustration
* Additional mentoring burden

### Culture

* Reduced accountability
* Toxic behavior

### Leadership Bandwidth

* Excessive performance management

A bad hire can consume more management time than hiring the right person takes.

# Hiring Framework

I use a structured evaluation process.

---

## Category 1: Technical Capability

Can they solve the problems expected at their level?

Evaluate:

* System Design
* Architecture
* Troubleshooting
* Automation
* Cloud Native Technologies

---

## Category 2: Problem Solving

Can they handle ambiguity?

Look for:

* Structured thinking
* Tradeoff analysis
* Root cause investigation

---

## Category 3: Ownership

Do they take responsibility?

Red Flags:

* Blaming others
* Avoiding accountability
* Excuse-driven answers

Positive Signals:

* "I owned..."
* "I learned..."
* "I fixed..."

---

## Category 4: Collaboration

Can they succeed in cross-functional environments?

Look for:

* Listening skills
* Communication
* Conflict resolution

---

## Category 5: Growth Mindset

Technology changes constantly.

I prefer candidates who learn quickly over candidates who know everything today.

# Scenario Question

## How Do You Hire Senior Engineers?

### Sample Answer

When hiring senior engineers, I focus less on tools and more on decision-making ability.

I evaluate:

* Technical depth
* Architecture thinking
* Stakeholder communication
* Ownership
* Mentorship capability

A senior engineer should improve the entire team, not just complete individual tasks.

For example, when evaluating platform engineers, I ask how they have influenced architecture decisions, handled incidents, and improved engineering productivity across teams.

---

# Scenario Question

## What Makes Someone a Strong Hire?

### Sample Answer

A strong hire demonstrates:

* Technical competence
* Curiosity
* Accountability
* Collaboration
* Learning agility

I look for evidence rather than claims.

Instead of asking whether someone is collaborative, I ask for examples of difficult stakeholder situations they have navigated.

Past behavior is often the best predictor of future behavior.

---

# Scenario Question

## Candidate A vs Candidate B

Candidate A

* Excellent technical skills
* Poor collaboration

Candidate B

* Good technical skills
* Excellent collaboration

Who would you hire?

---

# Sample Answer

My decision would depend on the current team needs.

However, in most cases I would lean toward Candidate B.

Technical skills can often be improved through mentoring and experience.

Collaboration issues frequently become organizational problems that affect multiple teams.

Developer Productivity teams succeed through influence rather than authority.

Strong collaboration skills are therefore critical.

I have successfully helped engineers improve technical capabilities, but repairing trust and collaboration issues is significantly harder.

---

# Scenario Question

## How Do You Avoid Hiring Bias?

### Sample Answer

I use structured interviews.

Key practices include:

* Standardized questions
* Consistent evaluation criteria
* Written feedback
* Independent scoring
* Diverse interview panels

The goal is to evaluate evidence rather than intuition.

This improves hiring quality and fairness.

# Scenario Question

## Tell Me About a Great Hiring Decision

### STAR Example

### Situation

A platform initiative required stronger Kubernetes and automation expertise.

### Task

Hire an engineer capable of improving platform reliability while collaborating across multiple teams.

### Action

During interviews, I focused not only on technical skills but also on ownership and communication.

One candidate demonstrated strong incident management experience and could clearly explain tradeoffs in previous architecture decisions.

### Result

The engineer became a key contributor, improved deployment automation, mentored junior team members, and significantly reduced operational overhead.

---

# Scenario Question

## Tell Me About a Hiring Mistake

### STAR Example

### Situation

Earlier in my career, I prioritized technical expertise heavily during hiring.

### Task

Fill a critical engineering role quickly.

### Action

I selected a candidate with exceptional technical depth.

However, I underestimated collaboration concerns raised during interviews.

### Result

The engineer delivered individually but created friction across teams.

The experience reinforced the importance of evaluating both technical and interpersonal competencies.

Since then, I use a more balanced hiring framework.

# Scenario Question

## One of Your Top Engineers Wants to Leave

### Sample Answer

First, I would understand why.

I would have an honest discussion about:

* Career goals
* Frustrations
* Expectations

If concerns are addressable, I would create a plan.

If they have already made a decision, I would support them professionally and ensure knowledge transfer.

Retention should never rely on pressure.

# Scenario Question

## What Happens If You Leave Tomorrow?

### Strong Answer

A successful manager should not be a single point of failure.

I work to document knowledge, distribute ownership, and develop future leaders.

My goal is that the team continues operating effectively even if I am unavailable.

That is one of the best indicators of leadership effectiveness.
