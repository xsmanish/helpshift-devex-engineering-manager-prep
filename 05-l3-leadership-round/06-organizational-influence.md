# Organizational Influence

## Helpshift Engineering Manager L3 Interview Preparation

---

# Why This Topic Matters

As an Engineering Manager, your authority becomes increasingly limited as your scope grows.

You cannot directly manage:

* Every engineer
* Every product manager
* Every security stakeholder
* Every executive
* Every engineering team

Yet you are still expected to drive outcomes.

This is where influence becomes one of the most important leadership skills.

For a Developer Productivity or Platform Engineering Manager role at Helpshift, success depends heavily on influence because:

* Platform teams serve other engineering teams.
* Adoption cannot be forced.
* Standards require buy-in.
* Engineering improvements require organizational alignment.

The best platform organizations win through influence, trust, credibility, and value creation.

---

# My Philosophy on Influence

## Core Belief

Authority can drive compliance.

Influence drives commitment.

Compliance lasts until supervision disappears.

Commitment creates lasting organizational change.

---

# Example

Imagine a platform team introduces a new CI/CD framework.

Teams can be forced to adopt it.

However, adoption will be slow, resistance will increase, and teams may build workarounds.

A better approach is:

* Understand pain points
* Build a better solution
* Demonstrate value
* Create champions
* Drive adoption organically

The outcome becomes significantly more sustainable.

---

# Sources of Influence

I believe influence comes from five areas:

```text id="5m3jv1"
Trust
→ Credibility
→ Relationships
→ Results
→ Consistency
```

---

# Trust

Trust is the foundation.

People follow leaders they trust.

Without trust:

* Recommendations are questioned.
* Change becomes difficult.
* Adoption slows.

Trust is built through:

* Transparency
* Reliability
* Integrity
* Consistency

---

# Credibility

People listen when they believe you understand the problem.

Credibility comes from:

* Technical expertise
* Business understanding
* Delivery track record

As a platform leader, credibility is often earned by solving real engineering problems.

---

# Relationships

Many organizational problems are relationship problems disguised as technical problems.

Strong relationships create:

* Faster alignment
* Easier conflict resolution
* Better collaboration

Relationship building should happen before problems appear.

---

# Results

Results create influence.

People support initiatives that demonstrate value.

Successful leaders repeatedly create positive outcomes.

---

# Consistency

Consistency creates predictability.

People trust leaders who behave consistently across situations.

---

# Scenario Question

## How Do You Influence Teams That Do Not Report To You?

This is one of the most common Developer Productivity interview questions.

---

# Sample Answer

I focus on understanding their goals before proposing solutions.

I start by asking:

* What challenges are they facing?
* What slows them down?
* What outcomes matter most?

Then I demonstrate how my team's solutions help them achieve those goals.

Influence becomes much easier when people see clear value.

Rather than pushing adoption, I focus on solving real problems.

---

# STAR Example

## Situation

Several engineering teams were using different deployment approaches.

This created operational complexity and inconsistent release practices.

---

## Task

Drive standardization without direct authority.

---

## Action

I met with team leads, understood their concerns, documented pain points, and demonstrated how GitOps could improve reliability and reduce operational overhead.

We piloted adoption with willing teams first.

Success stories were shared across the organization.

---

## Result

Adoption increased organically and platform standardization improved significantly.

The initiative succeeded because teams saw value rather than feeling forced.

---

# Scenario Question

## A Team Refuses To Use Your Platform

What Do You Do?

---

# Sample Answer

I would first understand why.

Resistance is often valuable feedback.

Questions I would ask:

* What concerns exist?
* What gaps exist?
* What alternatives are they using?
* What value are they missing?

The goal is not immediate compliance.

The goal is understanding adoption barriers.

Once root causes are understood, we can improve the platform or address misconceptions.

# Scenario Question

## A VP Wants Something Your Team Disagrees With

---

# Sample Answer

My responsibility is to provide informed recommendations rather than simply agree or disagree.

I would:

* Understand business objectives
* Evaluate tradeoffs
* Present risks
* Offer alternatives
* Recommend a path forward

If a final decision differs from my recommendation, I support execution while continuing to manage risks responsibly.

Leadership requires balancing advocacy with alignment.

---

# Example

Strong leaders do not say:

"That won't work."

Strong leaders say:

"Here are the risks, here are the tradeoffs, and here are alternative approaches."

# Executive Communication

One of the most overlooked leadership skills.

---

# How Executives Think

Executives care about:

* Business outcomes
* Risk
* Cost
* Time
* Customer impact

They rarely want implementation details.

---

# Example

Instead of saying:

"We need to upgrade Kubernetes clusters."

Say:

"This upgrade reduces security risk, improves reliability, and keeps us on a supported platform."

The second statement aligns with business priorities.

---

# Scenario Question

## How Do You Handle Resistance To Change?

---

# Sample Answer

I assume resistance contains useful information.

Rather than asking:

"Why are they resisting?"

I ask:

"What concerns are driving resistance?"

Common concerns include:

* Increased workload
* Risk
* Uncertainty
* Previous negative experiences

Understanding these concerns often reveals the path to adoption.

# Scenario Question

## How Do You Influence During An Incident?

---

During incidents, influence comes from clarity and calmness.

I focus on:

* Establishing ownership
* Prioritizing actions
* Communicating clearly
* Reducing confusion

People naturally align around leaders who create clarity under pressure.
